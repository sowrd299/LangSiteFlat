Re-framing Education Myths

Deficient-Oriented Education Myths

Deficit thinking is a frame of thought that places the blame of academic failure onto the individual student rather than the school/educational system. In the American educational system, native English-speakers are the dominant group for whom curriculum is designed. Non-native English-speakers, or English Language Learners, are the non-dominant group in this system. With deficit thinking, the experiential and cultural differences of non-dominant students are seen as a disadvantage that must be counteracted. Counteracting the disadvantages faced by English Language Learners assumes the following educational myths1:

  1. "At risk" children have a language problem. Their language and culture is deficient. They lack experiences. These deficits cause them to have learning problems.
  2. “At risk" children need to be separated from the regular class and need a structured program based on hierarchical notions of language development.
The American education system values monolingualism over bilingualism. This “has led to language-as-a-problem orientation, which is the view that the home language of a bilingual is an obstacle that a person needs to overcome in order to learn English. This leads to schools adapting programs that restrict student’s native language or use it very limited as the student transitions to the English language.”2 This is a subtractive approach to language arts education; English Language Development programs minimize students’ existing cultural and linguistic knowledge in favor of assimilating them to the dominant, English-speaking group. Instead of viewing English Language Learners as deficient, the US education system should encourage bilingualism in schools. A multicultural, bilingual approach to education assumes the following1:
  1. Children are proficient language users and bring many experiences into the classroom.
  2. Children need opportunities to learn language in rich, integrated settings and can be successful in regular classroom programs.
  3. The language development of these students can be effectively monitored by observing their language use in authentic settings across the curriculum.
With these assumptions in mind, education should shift to the “language-as-a-source orientation, which views one’s native language as a strength that can be used to help students learn English and other academic content.”2 Dual-immersion schools provide an environment in which bilingualism is fostered for both native English-speakers and English Language Learners. The result of a comprehensive and enriching multicultural education for all students is an all-inclusive class of emergent bilinguals.
