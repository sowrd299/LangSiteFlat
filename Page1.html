English Language Learners

What is an English Language Learner in the United States?

“English-language learners, or ELLs, are students who are unable to communicate fluently or learn effectively in English, who often come from non-English speaking homes and backgrounds, and who typically require specialized or modified instruction in both the English language and in their academic courses.” (edglossary.org)

The purpose of English Learning Development (ELD) in America

“English Language Development (ELD) is a systematic instructional model designed to develop the English language proficiency of English language learners (i.e., students who speak English as a second language)” (eldstrategies.com). These programs are designed to idolize English as the dominant language, thus marginalizing students who speak language(s) other than English in their homes. By putting these students in ELD programs, they are encouraging cultural determinism within schools; the foundational logic behind ELD programs blames the student’s culture and promotes the myth that, in order to succeed in America, one must be fluent in English. For example, in order to acculturate Native Americans to English-speaking American standards in the late 19th and early 20th century, Richard Pratt would place them into boarding schools where they were stripped of their language, culture, and identity. The goal with both Pratt’s strategies and ELD strategies is to assimilate students who belong to a non-dominant culture to the dominant English-speaking culture. Rather than embrace cultural difference, American educational institutions value acculturation, and the success of this process allows for greater control over the the once non-English speaking populace.

How do institutions plan to acculturate their ELL students?

In the American educational system, English Language Development (ELD) programs are in place to “fix” the problems that ELL students face. These programs segregate students into “special” classrooms, taking them away from their fundamental course classrooms to learn the most basic English skills. Even though ELD classes do not provide a robust language arts curriculum, these ELL students are still expected to function at the same level and possess the same skills as non-ELL students who are attending “normal” classes. In these remedial classes, students learn the bare minimum in order to pass their classes and graduate high school.

Do ELD programs prepare students for Higher Education?

ELD provides English Language Learners with basic English skills, but these programs do not prepare them for college or higher education programs. ELD programs are socially reproductive—that is, ELD perpetuates the idea that non-native English speakers are at a disadvantage and will achieve less than native speakers by teaching ELL students the basic knowledge they will need to maintain low-achieving status. Instead of providing ELL students with opportunities to surpass academic expectations, ELD repeatedly tests them on the same basic knowledge that they learned as children, thus undermining the students’ individual potential. One of the big issues for ELL students is that many aspire to go to college but have not been primed to do so. They are simply taught to pass their classes, not prepare for the college admissions process. As a method of social reproduction, ELD produces a cycle of English Language Learners who do not have the same opportunities for educational advancement and social mobility as their non-ELL peers.

ELD programs DO NOT promote bilingualism in education. ELD is optimally designed for beginners in English, assimilating them to the dominant, English-speaking culture.
